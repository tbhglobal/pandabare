# How to sleep on a plane in economy: a realistic guide

Here's the honest answer: you won't get a full night's sleep in economy, and aiming for one sets you up to be disappointed. What you can realistically get is 3 to 6 hours of light, broken sleep, if you manage cabin temperature, block light fully, support your neck, and pick the right window to sleep in. That's a genuine improvement over most people's flights, even if it isn't the eight hours you'd get at home.

If you've flown long-haul out of Australia, you already know the maths. Sydney to London is around 22 hours with a stopover. Perth to London can be done in one hop of over 17 hours. Even Melbourne to Los Angeles is close to 15 hours in the air. Australians do more long-haul economy flying than almost anyone else on earth, simply because of where we live. So this guide skips the business class fantasy tips and focuses on what actually helps when you're in a 43cm-wide seat for most of a day.

## Why is it so hard to sleep on a plane in economy?

It's not just the seat. Cabin pressure at cruising altitude is equivalent to an altitude of roughly 6,000 to 8,000 feet, according to research published in the [New England Journal of Medicine](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa062770). That slightly lowers the oxygen in your blood, which makes it harder to reach deep, restorative sleep stages.

On top of that, cabin humidity typically sits at 10 to 20 percent, drier than most deserts. Combine that with cabin noise, meal service, turbulence and an upright seat back, and you get a body that's being told, in several different ways at once, that this isn't a safe place to properly switch off.

There's also a compounding effect. Each time you're woken (a trolley, a seatmate, turbulence) it can take 15 to 30 minutes to drift back into sleep. On an overnight flight with several service rounds, that adds up to a lot of lost time, even if you were "asleep" for most of the flight.

None of this means sleep is impossible. It means the goal should be reducing disruptions and improving sleep quality within a genuinely difficult environment, not chasing a perfect night.

## What's the best sleeping position for economy class?

There's no single best position, but some do noticeably better than others.

- **Window seat, leaning into the wall**: gives you a stable surface for your head and control over the window shade, which matters more than people expect for daytime departures out of Australia.
- **Slight recline (roughly 15 to 20 degrees) with head supported on both sides**: reduces the distance your head drops before your neck muscles kick in and wake you.
- **Avoid slumping forward**: it feels like less effort in the moment but tends to wake you faster and can leave your neck sore for days.
- **Middle or aisle seat**: without a wall, a firm travel pillow doing the job of both sides of your neck becomes far more important.

If you're flying overnight from Australia (most Europe and US routes), try to shift into "sleep mode" close to your normal bedtime rather than the moment the seatbelt sign turns off. If you're on a daytime flight, don't force sleep. Rest with your eyes closed and let a real sleep window happen later, ideally timed to your destination.

## Do neck pillows actually work?

The evidence here is genuinely mixed, not a clear yes. Systematic reviews of cervical and travel pillow studies show inconsistent results across different pillow designs, and no single style has been shown to clearly outperform the others for sleep quality (see [this systematic review](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40633255/) and the associated [meta-analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0268003321000838)). Some designs, particularly firmer, more contoured shapes, show moderate evidence of reducing waking symptoms and improving perceived comfort.

Translated to a plane seat: a neck pillow won't manufacture deep sleep out of nowhere. What a decent one can do is stop your head from lolling forward or sideways, which reduces the number of times you jolt awake. On a long flight, fewer wake-ups is a real, measurable win, even without a guarantee of better sleep architecture.

## Should I take melatonin before or during a flight?

Melatonin is worth understanding as a jet lag tool, not a sleeping pill. [Harvard Health](https://www.health.harvard.edu/sleep/melatonin-for-jet-lag) and a review in [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8958662/) both note that melatonin taken close to your destination's target bedtime, for a few nights around travel, can help reset your body clock after crossing several time zones.

It won't sedate you or override 14 hours of upright, dry, noisy cabin air. If you use it, timing matters: taken at the wrong time of day it can shift your body clock in the wrong direction. If you're considering any sleep medication for a flight, it's worth a quick conversation with your GP or pharmacist beforehand, especially for longer routes from Australia where you're crossing many zones at once.

## What actually helps you sleep on a plane (a realistic checklist)

This is less about buying every gadget in the airport lounge and more about controlling the handful of things you actually can.

| Stage | What to do | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| Before boarding | Choose a window seat if sleep is the priority | Gives you a stable lean and shade control |
| Before boarding | Avoid alcohol and heavy meals pre-flight | Both worsen dehydration and disrupt sleep stages in low-humidity cabins |
| Before boarding | Pack a sleep mask, socks and a travel pillow in your carry-on | You want these within reach, not in the overhead locker |
| Take-off | Set expectations: aim for broken sleep, not a full night | Reduces frustration and the anxiety of "trying" to sleep |
| In-flight | Block light fully with a well-fitted sleep mask | Light exposure is one of the strongest signals telling your body to stay awake |
| In-flight | Keep feet and lower legs warm with bamboo socks | Cabin air is cool and dry; cold feet noticeably delay sleep onset |
| In-flight | Recline slightly and support both sides of your neck | Reduces the head-drop that triggers frequent waking |
| In-flight | Drink water regularly, skip extra caffeine and alcohol | Counters cabin dehydration, which worsens fatigue and headaches |
| In-flight | Get up and walk every few hours on very long sectors | Helps circulation and reduces stiffness on 14+ hour flights |
| After landing | Get outside into daylight as soon as you can | Daylight exposure helps reset your circadian rhythm faster than sleep alone |

## How do I handle overnight vs daytime long-haul flights from Australia?

Route timing changes your strategy more than most guides admit.

On an **overnight flight** (common on Perth-Europe or east coast-Middle East routes), sleep early once meal service finishes, keep your mask on through any remaining light periods, and treat any wake-ups as brief rather than a signal to give up and watch a film.

On a **daytime flight** (common on Australia-US west coast routes), don't fight your body clock. Rest, hydrate, and save your main sleep effort for the destination's first night, using light exposure and timing to help you adjust once you land.

Either way, the goal for the flight itself is the same: reduce the number of disruptions, keep your body temperature and light exposure steady, and accept that light, broken sleep is a genuine win in economy.

## FAQ

**What is the best sleeping position for economy class?**
A slight recline of around 15 to 20 degrees with your head supported on both sides tends to work best. A window seat lets you lean into the wall. In a middle or aisle seat, a firm travel pillow that supports both sides of your neck does a similar job.

**Do neck pillows actually work on planes?**
The evidence is mixed rather than strongly positive. Studies on cervical and travel pillows show inconsistent results, but a firm, contoured pillow that stops your head from dropping forward or sideways does reduce the number of times you jolt awake, which is the real goal on a plane.

**Should I take melatonin to sleep on a flight?**
Melatonin is better understood as a jet lag tool than a sleeping pill. It can help shift your body clock on multi-day trips, taken close to your destination's target bedtime, but it will not make economy seating comfortable or guarantee sleep on the flight itself.

**Why is it so hard to sleep on a plane even when you're exhausted?**
Cabin pressure at cruising altitude is equivalent to being at roughly 6,000 to 8,000 feet, which lowers blood oxygen slightly and makes deep sleep harder to reach. Add low humidity, cabin noise, meal service and an upright seat, and your body simply doesn't get the conditions it needs for continuous sleep.

**How many hours of real sleep can I expect on a long-haul flight?**
On a 14 to 22 hour flight from Australia, most people get somewhere between 3 and 6 hours of broken, light sleep rather than one continuous block. Treating that as a realistic target, instead of aiming for a full night, makes the whole flight less frustrating.

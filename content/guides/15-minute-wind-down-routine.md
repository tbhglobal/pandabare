# The 15 minute wind-down routine for people who can't switch off

A realistic wind-down routine takes 15 minutes: phone away, lights dimmed, a couple of minutes of slow breathing or stretching, then a fixed final cue like sleepwear, a mask or socks. Done most nights, this teaches your brain to link those small actions with sleep, which matters more than any single step.

If you're the kind of person who lies in bed replaying the day's Slack messages, you don't need a 90 minute spa ritual. You need something short enough that you'll actually do it on a Tuesday night after a long commute. This is that routine.

## Why can't I switch off at night?

Your brain doesn't have an off switch, and it isn't supposed to. Sleep researchers at [Sleep Matters Perth](https://www.sleepmattersperth.com.au/winding-down-for-sleep/) note that many people mistakenly believe "switching off" means shutting the mind down completely, when really the goal is calmer thoughts, not no thoughts.

The problem for most Australians isn't a broken brain. It's a day that ends abruptly: last email at 9pm, phone in hand until lights out, then an expectation that sleep will arrive on command. There's no transition, so your nervous system doesn't get the memo.

A wind-down routine is that transition. It's a deliberate set of low-stimulation steps between "still working" and "asleep."

## What does a realistic 15 minute wind-down routine look like?

Most wind-down advice assumes you have an hour to spare for a bath, candles and a chapter of fiction. Realistically, most people don't. Here's a routine that fits in 15 minutes and still covers the parts that matter: light, screens, body, and a repeatable cue.

| Minutes | Step | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| 0 to 2 | Phone goes on charge outside arm's reach, main lights dimmed or switched to a lamp | Reduces blue light exposure and stops the scroll-until-you're-tired trap |
| 2 to 5 | Lay out tomorrow's essentials (keys, bag, gym clothes) or jot down 3 things on your mind | Clears the "did I forget something" loop that keeps people awake |
| 5 to 8 | Change into sleepwear, brush teeth, splash face with water | Builds a physical cue that your brain starts to associate with sleep |
| 8 to 11 | Slow stretch or 4 rounds of box breathing (in 4, hold 4, out 4, hold 4) | Lowers physical tension and heart rate before you lie down |
| 11 to 14 | Get into bed, put on a sleep mask, socks on if your feet run cold | Fixed final cue: the same last action every night, signalling lights out |
| 14 to 15 | No phone, eyes closed, let your mind wander without chasing any one thought | Gives your brain permission to drift rather than problem-solve |

## How do you build a wind-down cue your brain recognises?

This is the part most routines skip. A wind-down routine only works well if it's repeated, because repetition is what turns a set of actions into a cue. The [Sleep Health Foundation](https://www.sleephealthfoundation.org.au/sleep-topics/sleep-hygiene-good-sleep-habits) points out that your internal clock and sleep hormones work best with a regular routine, and that following the same steps each night, like changing into pyjamas or brushing your teeth, reinforces to your brain that it's bedtime.

The mechanism is straightforward: if you do the same three or four things every single night before sleep, your brain starts to expect sleep once it recognises the pattern. It's the same principle as a toddler's bedtime routine, just for adults who'd rather not admit they need one.

Pick one small, fixed final action and don't change it. For a lot of people that's putting on a sleep mask to block light and signal "we're done for the day." For others it's pulling on a pair of bed socks because cold feet are the last thing keeping them awake. Whatever you choose, the value is in doing the exact same thing, most nights, not in the object itself.

## Does screen time before bed really matter?

Yes, but the mechanism is more specific than "screens are bad." Blue light in the 460 to 480 nanometre range hits photosensitive cells in your eyes that signal your brain's master clock to hold back melatonin release, the hormone that makes you feel sleepy, according to a review summarised by [UCLA Health](https://www.uclahealth.org/news/article/block-screens-blue-light-to-get-better-sleep). Normally melatonin rises in the evening; blue light exposure delays that rise and can push out how long it takes you to fall asleep.

Research on blue-light-blocking glasses is genuinely mixed. Some trials show a modest improvement in sleep onset and melatonin levels, while a [systematic review published in Frontiers in Neurology](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/neurology/articles/10.3389/fneur.2025.1699303/full) found the effect on objective sleep measures was inconsistent across studies. The more reliable fix isn't a pair of glasses, it's simply putting the phone down. General guidance from sleep researchers is to stop using light-emitting devices at least 30 to 60 minutes before bed, which also removes the mental stimulation of scrolling, arguably the bigger sleep thief.

For Australians doing shift work or coming off a late commute, this is harder. If you can't avoid a screen right before bed, at minimum dim the brightness, switch on night mode, and keep the last 10 minutes screen-free no matter what.

## How long should it actually take to fall asleep?

Most healthy adults fall asleep within 10 to 20 minutes of getting into bed, based on data summarised by the [Sleep Foundation](https://www.sleepfoundation.org/how-sleep-works/sleep-latency). Falling asleep in under 5 minutes most nights can actually be a sign of sleep debt rather than great sleep hygiene. Taking longer than 30 minutes regularly, in a way that affects how you feel the next day, meets the general threshold clinicians look at for insomnia and is worth raising with a GP.

A wind-down routine won't turn a 45 minute sleep onset into 10 minutes overnight. What it reliably does is remove the self-inflicted delays: the extra 20 minutes lost to scrolling, the racing mind because nothing signalled "day's over." Fixing those gets you back into the normal range.

## What if you're a shift worker or your schedule changes constantly?

The routine matters more, not less, when your schedule is irregular. Daylight, traffic noise and a body clock expecting to be awake all work against you. A [sleep mask](/products/sleep-mask) blocks the light cue your brain would otherwise use to stay alert, which is particularly useful if you're trying to sleep at 10am after a night shift.

The fix is the same 15 minute structure, just triggered by "about to sleep" rather than "it's night time." Keep the steps identical regardless of the clock: same lighting change, same final cue, same order. Your brain doesn't read a clock, it reads patterns.

## FAQ

**What is the best 15 minute wind-down routine before bed?**
Put your phone away first, dim the lights, then layer in a physical cue like changing into sleepwear and a short stretch or breathing exercise. Finish with a fixed last step, such as a sleep mask or bed socks, done the same way most nights.

**Does screen time before bed actually stop you from sleeping?**
Blue light can suppress melatonin and delay your body's sleepy signal, but the effect is modest for most people. The bigger issue is usually the mental stimulation of scrolling, not the light itself.

**How long should it normally take to fall asleep?**
Most healthy adults take 10 to 20 minutes. Under 5 minutes regularly can signal sleep debt, and over 30 minutes regularly is worth mentioning to a GP.

**What if I only have 5 minutes, not 15?**
Do the last three steps only: phone away, lights dimmed, and your fixed sleep cue. A short routine done every night beats a long one done occasionally.

**Does a wind-down routine actually work for shift workers?**
Yes, and it matters more, because the environment won't naturally cue sleep at odd hours. Keep the same steps in the same order regardless of what time your "bedtime" is, and use a sleep mask to block daylight.

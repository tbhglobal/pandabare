# Corporate gifts clients keep: a practical guide for Australian businesses

The corporate gifts clients keep are the ones that solve a small, real problem: warm feet, better sleep, an easier flight. In Australia, that means comfort essentials (quality socks, a sleep mask, a travel pouch) tend to outlast branded pens, stress balls and mugs. Pick usefulness over logo size, time it well, and check the tax treatment with your accountant before you claim it.

That is the short version. Here is the practical detail: what actually gets kept, how to budget it, when to send it, and how to think about the EOFY and Christmas rush without either eco-guilt or a hard sell.

## Why do most corporate gifts end up in a drawer or the bin?

Most corporate gifts fail for one reason: they were chosen for the giver, not the recipient.

A pen with your logo tells a client about your business. A pair of genuinely comfortable socks tells them you thought about their day. Industry research on promotional products backs this up: quality is what determines whether an item gets used or discarded, and [72% of people say the quality of a promotional item reflects on the company that gave it](https://www.giftafeeling.com/pages/promotional-product-statistics-2025). Recipients also report only considering a gift a success if they actually use it, not just receive it.

The fix is simple. Give something that would earn a place in someone's home or travel bag anyway, then add your brand quietly rather than loudly.

## What corporate gifts do Australian clients actually keep and use?

The gifts that survive past the unboxing moment share three traits: they are useful daily or weekly, they feel considered rather than mass-produced, and the branding is restrained.

Comfort essentials tick all three. A pair of [bamboo crew socks](/products/bamboo-crew-socks), a [sleep mask](/products/sleep-mask) for frequent flyers, or a [travel pouch](/products/travel-pouch) packed with small essentials all get used long after a stress ball has been binned.

## Branded merchandise or useful gifts: which wins with clients?

This is the real decision most gifting programs get wrong, and it is worth being direct about it.

Heavily branded merchandise (the kind covered edge to edge in a logo) signals marketing spend, not appreciation. It is also the category most likely to be thrown away, since the item's usefulness is usually secondary to the branding. A shift toward quality over quantity in corporate gifting has been widely reported, with more companies choosing fewer, better items over large runs of low-cost branded stock, as covered by [Stacker's reporting on the end of throwaway corporate swag](https://krdo.com/stacker-money/2026/01/26/the-end-of-throwaway-corporate-swag-why-companies-are-investing-in-quality-over-quantity/).

The alternative is not "no branding". It is proportional branding: a small embroidered mark on a genuinely good product, rather than a logo that dominates the item. This is where comfort essentials do well. A soft bamboo sock or a neatly packed travel pouch can carry a subtle brand tag without looking like a marketing handout.

## When is the best time to send corporate gifts in Australia?

Two windows dominate Australian business gifting: Christmas (which falls in summer here) and the end of financial year on 30 June.

Christmas is the most common gifting moment, but it is also the most crowded. Your gift arrives alongside a dozen others on the same desk in the same week. EOFY, by contrast, is a genuinely underused window: it lands mid-year, avoids the December pile-up, and pairs naturally with a "thank you for the year so far" message rather than a generic holiday card.

A third option worth considering is milestone-based gifting: sending something when a project wraps, a contract renews, or a referral comes through. These moments carry more personal weight than a calendar date, and a well-timed comfort gift (a [sleep mask](/products/sleep-mask) after a big travel-heavy project, for instance) reads as considered rather than automatic.

If your client base travels frequently for work, our guide to [long-haul flight comfort kits](/guides/long-haul-flight-comfort-kit) is a useful reference for building a travel-focused gift that a busy client will genuinely use.

## Are corporate gifts to clients tax deductible in Australia?

This is a common question, and it is worth answering carefully rather than confidently, because the rules genuinely depend on the specifics of the gift and the relationship.

In general terms, the distinction that matters is entertainment versus non-entertainment. Non-entertainment gifts, given with the clear purpose of generating future business, such as a hamper, a branded item, or a gift voucher, tend to be treated differently to entertainment-style gifts like event tickets, concerts, or client dinners, which are often not deductible. You can read the ATO's own framing of entertainment scenarios for business on the [ATO website](https://www.ato.gov.au/businesses-and-organisations/hiring-and-paying-your-workers/fringe-benefits-tax/types-of-fringe-benefits/entertainment-related-fringe-benefits/common-entertainment-scenarios-for-business), and general guidance on the deductibility question from firms like [Wood Accounting](https://woodaccounting.com.au/are-client-gifts-tax-deductible-we-break-it-down-for-you/) and the [Small Business Development Corporation](https://www.smallbusiness.wa.gov.au/blog/claiming-tax-deductions-client-gifts).

None of this is tax advice, and it should not be treated as a final answer for your business. Keep your receipts, note down why the gift relates to generating future income, and confirm your specific position with your accountant or the current ATO guidance before you claim anything.

## FAQ

**What are corporate gifts clients actually keep, rather than bin?**
Clients keep things they would have bought for themselves: comfort items, quality basics, and anything genuinely useful at home, in the office or on a flight. Heavily logoed items rank lowest for long-term use, while subtle branding on premium, everyday essentials performs far better.

**Are client gifts tax deductible in Australia?**
This is genuinely nuanced and depends on your specific circumstances, so treat this as general information, not tax advice. In broad terms, non-entertainment gifts given to generate future business are more commonly deductible than entertainment-style gifts such as event tickets or dinners, but you should confirm your position with your accountant or current ATO guidance before claiming anything.

**When should Australian businesses send corporate gifts: Christmas or EOFY?**
Christmas remains the busiest gifting window, which also means client inboxes and desks are flooded with gifts at the same time. Sending around EOFY (30 June) or after a project milestone can help your gift stand out and land at a genuinely useful moment.

**Is branded merchandise still worth giving to clients?**
Branded merchandise works best when the item itself has value beyond the logo. A well-made, comfortable essential with a small, tasteful brand mark tends to get kept and used, while heavily branded, low-quality items are more likely to be discarded.

**How much should a business budget for a client gift in Australia?**
Most Australian businesses sit somewhere between $20 and $150 per client gift, depending on the relationship and occasion. A thoughtful $40 to $60 comfort-focused gift often reads as more considered than a $100 item with a heavy branding footprint.
